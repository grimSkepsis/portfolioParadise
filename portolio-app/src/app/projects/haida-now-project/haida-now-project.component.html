The Brief

  • As part of a collaborative effort between SFU and the Museum of Vancouver, a series of short documentaries were being made as part of an exhibit about the Haida, an indigenous people native to British Columbia. My team was tasked to interview two of the curators exhibit.

Haida Now was an exciting project where my team and I worked closely with Haida curator and artist Kwi Jones and MOV Curator Viviane Gosselin to highlight the Museum's role in the reconciliation process.

Cleaning the Audio, an Exercise in Insanity

Bad Audio

Better Audio

Our audio from the initial interview was subject to several abject horrors including but not limited to lavalier mic rustling, very noticeable background noise from exhibits, and the droning of an angry air conditioner. After a lot of experimentation, research, and sleepless nights I was able to clean it to a level that would sound clean on most speakers.

Adjusting, Adjusting, Adjusting

Our film was all over the place lighting and color palette wise so color correction was a grueling team experience. My partner and I co-piloted this process in order to bring everything to a more neutral palette. I then went back through and added a final adjustment layer to make our film a touch more cinegrapic and blend together some of our more color contrasted scenes.

Reflection

This project was a really valuable lesson in terms of collaborating with a community partner and getting to bring to light a compelling story on subject matter that was completely alien to me prior. Moving forward I have a new found appreciation for the variety of camera techniques and shot types that are possible and the narrative impact of each, as well as a deepened respect for audio editing and the value clean audio brings to a project.
